2. What Is an IEPF Claim?
The Investor Education and Protection Fund, or IEPF, is a statutory fund under the Ministry of Corporate Affairs. When dividends on a company’s shares remain unpaid or unclaimed for seven consecutive years, the unpaid dividends and the related shares are generally transferred to the IEPF. The money or securities do not disappear, but the investor or rightful claimant must follow the prescribed refund process to get them back.
An IEPF claim is therefore the process of applying for the return of shares, dividends, matured deposits, debentures, or other eligible amounts that have already been moved to the fund.
Many families discover the issue late — after a death, while sorting old papers, or when dividend letters surface against an outdated address or bank account.

9. Frequently Asked Questions
Can shares be transferred to IEPF even if I still hold a demat account?
Yes. In some cases the shares may remain in demat form but dividends linked to an inactive or outdated bank mandate remain unclaimed. In many other cases both the unpaid dividends and the related shares are transferred after the statutory period. The exact status has to be checked from the records.
What is the basic process for an IEPF refund claim?
At a high level, the claimant files Form IEPF-5, submits the relevant document set to the company’s Nodal Officer, the company verifies the claim and sends its report, and the authority processes the refund or credit if the claim is accepted.
Can legal heirs file an IEPF claim?
Yes. Legal heirs, nominees, successors, and families can file, but the case usually requires additional proof of entitlement and, in some situations, succession documentation.
Can NRIs claim shares and dividends from IEPF?
Yes. NRIs can claim, but they may need extra identity and entitlement support depending on the facts and the documents available.
How long does an IEPF claim take?
There is no single reliable timeline. Clean cases move faster than cases involving death, missing papers, mismatches, or clarification requests.
Do I need all documents before I even ask for help?
No. It is better to start with whatever you have. A realistic review can often identify what is missing and what can be rebuilt.

2. What Is This Service?
Transmission of shares is the process of changing the ownership record after the death of a shareholder so the holding can pass to the person or persons legally entitled to it. The claimant may be a registered nominee, surviving joint holder, executor under a will, administrator, or legal heir proceeding under the route accepted for that case.
Many families confuse transmission with transfer. The difference matters. A transfer is a voluntary movement of ownership between living parties. Transmission happens because of death and follows a succession-based entitlement route. Treating the two as the same often causes delay, wrong paperwork, or a complete misclassification of the case.
Transmission can apply to physical shares, demat holdings, or fragmented legacy holdings spread across multiple companies or registrars. In some cases it is the central task. In others it is the first step before dematerialisation, sale, IEPF recovery, or correction of old record mismatches.
3. When Does This Issue Usually Arise?
• A shareholder dies and the family later discovers shares in physical or demat form.
• The family knows investments existed, but the deceased holder is still shown on the record and the shares cannot be sold, dematerialised, or dealt with.
• There is no nomination on record, or the nomination does not fully solve the practical entitlement issue.
• Multiple heirs are involved and the family is unsure which legal route applies.
• The holding surfaces only during estate clean-up, succession planning, locker review, or after old papers are found.
• Transmission needs to happen before a linked problem such as duplicate documents, physical-to-demat conversion, or IEPF recovery can move forward.

2. What Does Transmission-cum-Demat Mean?
Transmission-cum-demat is the route used when paper share certificates stand in the name of a deceased holder and the claimant wants the holding moved into a legally usable demat account.
In plain English, two things have to happen together. First, the right person or persons must be recognised as entitled to the shares after the holder’s death. Second, the old paper certificates must be converted into electronic holdings. Until both layers are handled properly, the investment often remains stuck.
This is why families frequently get confused. They think they are dealing with one task, but the case actually combines succession, entitlement proof, record verification, and dematerialisation. If any one of those pieces is weak, the entire file can slow down or stop.
3. When Does This Issue Usually Arise?
• A family discovers old physical share certificates after the original holder has died.

9. Frequently Asked Questions
What is the difference between transmission, demat, and transmission-cum-demat?
Transmission changes the ownership record after death. Demat converts paper certificates into electronic form. Transmission-cum-demat is used when both have to happen together because the certificates are physical and the holder is deceased.
Can we proceed if the original holder died many years ago?
Often yes, but older cases may need more work because documents scatter, family records weaken, and correction issues accumulate around the holding.
What if we have the certificates but no nominee details?
That does not automatically end the case. It usually means the entitlement route has to be built through the applicable heir or succession documentation instead of relying on nominee status.
Can NRIs inherit and regularise old physical shares from abroad?
Yes, but the execution has to be handled carefully. Identity documents, attestation trail, entitlement proof, and receiving demat-account alignment all matter.
What if the names on the old certificates do not match current IDs exactly?
That is common. It usually means the file may also need record-correction support instead of being treated as a clean transmission-cum-demat request.
Can the family sell the shares immediately after finding the certificates?
Not usually. First the ownership and format issues have to be regularised properly. Only then does the holding become practically usable.
