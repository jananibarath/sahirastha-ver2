3. Frequently Asked Questions
How do I find out if I have unclaimed money in India?
Start by thinking asset class, not just institution. Old money in India does not sit in one place. It may be lying in an old PF account, a dormant bank deposit, unpaid insurance proceeds, unclaimed mutual fund redemption or dividend amounts, old shares, or shares and dividends transferred to IEPF. That means the search route changes depending on what kind of money you are trying to locate.
A practical search usually begins with the clues you already have: PAN, old bank statements, email trails, policy papers, employer name, folio numbers, share certificates, passbooks, salary slips, and death records if the search is after a family member’s death. Public search and trace points matter here. RBI’s UDGAM portal helps search certain unclaimed bank deposits. AMFI and MF Central surface mutual-fund tracing tools including MITRA and unclaimed dividend or redemption checks. Insurers provide unclaimed-amount search through their own portals and IRDAI’s Bima Bharosa points users to those routes. IEPF applies where dividends and shares have already moved out of the company’s hands. In other words, the first job is not “claim everything at once”. It is “identify what exists, where it sits, and under whose name it is still traceable”.
How do I find all financial assets of a deceased family member in India?
This is usually not one search. It is a reconstruction exercise. Families often expect a single certificate or a single complaint to reveal everything. Real life is messier. A deceased person may have had PF from more than one employer, old mutual fund folios, insurance policies, bank deposits, physical shares, demat holdings, or IEPF-transferred assets. Some may be active, some dormant, and some already in a regulator-managed pool or suspense state.
The practical starting point is evidence collection. Look at bank statements for premium payments, SIPs, dividend credits, salary references, and maturity payouts that never reached the claimant. Check email inboxes, tax records, Form 16, passbooks, salary slips, old file covers, policy bonds, agent correspondence, cheque counterfoils, demat statements, and SMS alerts. Then search asset-by-asset. Death creates two separate tasks: tracing the asset and proving the claimant position. Those are not the same thing. You may successfully locate an asset and still face nomination, heirship, transmission, KYC, or succession issues before it can move.
Is a nominee the legal owner?
Very often, no — or at least not in the simplistic way people assume. “Nominee” is one of the most misunderstood words in Indian financial recovery matters. In many contexts, nomination helps the institution decide whom it can deal with for payment or transmission. That does not automatically mean all beneficial ownership questions vanish. In bank deposits, RBI guidance makes clear that payment to a nominee can discharge the bank’s liability, but the nominee receives the money as a trustee for the legal heirs. In mutual funds, AMFI states that nomination does not by itself create beneficial title; the nominee may receive as agent or trustee for the legal heirs or legatees.
Insurance adds further nuance because the effect of nomination can depend on the policy and the statutory framework. The practical lesson is simple: nomination is important, but it is not magic. It may speed a clean case. It may also coexist with legal-heir, family, or succession questions. Anyone giving blanket answers without asking which asset class is involved is usually simplifying past the truth.
What is the difference between nominee, legal heir, successor, and beneficiary?
A nominee is the person recorded in the institution’s records to receive or handle the asset on the occurrence of a trigger such as death. A legal heir is a person who inherits under the applicable succession law when the owner dies. A successor is a broader term often used to describe the person who steps into the rights of the deceased. A beneficiary is the person ultimately entitled to receive the economic benefit under contract, nomination, will, or succession, depending on the context.
The problem is that people use these words as if they were interchangeable. They are not. In some clean cases the same person may fit all roles. In many cases they do not. A widow may be the nominee. Adult children may also be legal heirs. A will may name one person while institutional records show another. A bank may be willing to pay the nominee, but a later family dispute may still arise outside the bank. That is why proper classification matters before documents are filed or family members start signing no-objection papers blindly.
When do I need a legal heir certificate, succession certificate, probate, or NOC from family members?
There is no one answer because institutions do not ask for the same thing in the same situation. A valid nomination or survivorship clause may simplify settlement in some bank and insurance cases. Clean mutual fund or PF cases may also move differently from contested or no-nomination cases. But once there is no valid nominee, a death-related dispute, multiple heirs, a will-based claim, or a high-friction institutional review, formal proof of claimant status becomes far more important.
The blunt truth is that people often waste months by collecting the wrong certificate first. A legal heir certificate, succession certificate, probate, letter of administration, indemnity set, NOC set, or family declaration may each have their place, but the route depends on the asset type, the value, the institution, the nomination status, and whether the death holder’s title itself is clear. Good case handling means asking not just “what document do they want?” but “what legal and institutional question is this document supposed to solve?”
How do I check if I have old shares or unpaid dividends in my name?
Start with whatever evidence survived: physical certificates, folio numbers, old dividend warrants, annual reports, company letters, RTA communication, demat statements, or even family memory of company names. Then separate the situation into two possibilities. One, the shares or unpaid dividends still sit with the company or its registrar. Two, the unpaid dividend continued long enough for the underlying shares to be transferred to IEPF. Those are different routes.
Before IEPF transfer, company investor relations pages, registrar records, folio details, and unpaid dividend notices matter. After IEPF transfer, the route moves into IEPF claim territory. A common mistake is to jump to IEPF without first checking whether the shares are still traceable at company level. Another common mistake is to ignore the death question. If the original holder is deceased, you are not dealing only with “old shares”. You are dealing with transmission, entitlement, and possibly then IEPF recovery.
How do I claim shares or dividends from IEPF?
An IEPF claim is usually initiated online through web-form IEPF-5. It is not a casual email exercise. The form is PAN-verified, and the claimant has to provide company details, claimant details, bank and demat details where relevant, and the supporting documents required for the specific claim. In death-related or transmission cases, the document burden is heavier because the claimant must prove not only that the asset exists but also that the claimant is entitled to receive it.
The practical sequence matters. In many cases, especially where the original shareholder is deceased, transmission issues have to be addressed with the company or registrar before the IEPF refund can move cleanly. The institution-side verification report is also part of the process. So when people ask whether IEPF is “just one form”, the honest answer is no. The form is only the visible front end. The real work often sits in entitlement proof, document quality, and whether the record trail can survive scrutiny.
What if the original shareholder has died and the shares are now in IEPF?
This is one of the cases where people lose time by treating two problems as one. A death case with IEPF usually involves both transmission logic and IEPF refund logic. The claimant must first establish the relationship to the deceased holder and deal with the company-side or registrar-side entitlement trail. Depending on the facts, that may involve death certificate, heirship or succession papers, indemnity or NOC sets, demat details, and the exact folio or share details that moved into IEPF.
The hard part is not merely filling a form. The hard part is building a coherent entitlement file that the company, registrar, and IEPF ecosystem will all accept. If records are weak, names differ across documents, or there are multiple heirs without alignment, this stops being a routine refund and becomes a document-architecture problem.
How do I find old LIC or insurance policies and unclaimed insurance money?
Begin with the insurer if you know it. Insurers maintain unclaimed-amount search routes, and IRDAI’s Bima Bharosa unclaimed-amount query points users to the company-level portals. Search inputs commonly include policyholder name, date of birth, PAN, and sometimes policy number. If you do not have the policy number, reconstruct the trail from bank statements, premium entries, agent contact, employer records, email confirmations, physical policy bonds, or family files.
Do not treat every insurance case as the same. Unclaimed insurance money may be a maturity amount, survival benefit, death claim, annuity payout, or an amount blocked because of address, bank, or KYC mismatch. The search step tells you whether something is traceable. The next step is to classify what exactly it is, because the documents and route can change sharply from there.
How do I claim life insurance after the death of the policyholder?
The claimant usually has to notify the insurer, submit the claim form, provide death certificate, identity and address proof of the claimant, bank proof, and policy details, along with any event-specific documents the insurer asks for. If the death is accidental or medically sensitive, additional records may be requested. The cleanest cases are those where the nomination is valid, policy details are available, and claimant identity matches the insurer’s records well enough for payout.
However, many real cases are not clean. Old policies, missing bonds, changed address, bank mismatch, rival family claims, assignment issues, or uncertainty about who is entitled can slow the process. IRDAI’s policyholder framework sets timelines for decision-making once all relevant papers are received, but the phrase that matters is “all relevant papers”. People hear the headline timeline and forget that incomplete or contested records postpone the real starting point.
What if maturity amount or survival benefit was never received?
This usually means the money did not fail because the policy did not exist. It failed because the payout could not complete properly or was never claimed properly. Common reasons include old address or bank details, un-updated KYC, returned cheques, failed ECS credit, untracked maturity communication, old LIC or insurer record mismatch, policy loan effects, or confusion about whether the policy matured, lapsed, or remained in force.
The correct approach is to verify three things in sequence: whether the policy was valid up to the relevant date, whether the benefit became due, and what blocked payment. People often jump directly to “where is my money?” when the institution is still sitting at “whose bank account, whose KYC, and under what exact payout category?” That classification gap is where many survival-benefit cases stall.
How do I check if there is old PF money in my name?
If you have your UAN and access to the EPFO member systems, the search may begin there. But many people searching for old PF do not have a clean digital trail. They changed jobs years ago, never consolidated accounts, forgot old member IDs, or worked in establishments where records were weak. In those cases, old payslips, PF numbers, employer names, Form 16, salary bank credits, and joining-exit dates become the reconstruction inputs.
It is also important to separate three situations: an active EPF balance, an old balance left behind after employment changes, and a death-related PF entitlement. Another complication is that not every establishment sits identically inside EPFO’s direct structure. Some are exempted trusts. So the right first question is not only “how much PF do I have?” but “which employer period, which PF member ID, and which system currently holds the record?”
How do I claim PF after leaving a job?
This depends on whether you are withdrawing, transferring, or cleaning up an old account trail. EPFO’s claim architecture includes forms and routes such as final settlement by member and transfer from old account to new one. In practice, the key blockers are usually not the form names. They are whether exit details were updated, whether UAN and KYC are aligned, whether bank and Aadhaar records match, and whether the member is dealing with one clean account or several fragmented ones.
Many people search “how to claim PF after leaving job” when the better question is “should I withdraw, transfer, or first correct the record?” A route mistake here can create tax confusion, pension confusion, or a longer correction trail later. The process is therefore part compliance and part record diagnosis.
How do I claim PF of a deceased employee?
Death-related PF is not just one claim. Depending on the facts, the family may be looking at EPF balance, pension-related benefits, and EDLI insurance components. EPFO’s public material identifies Form 20 for final settlement in favour of nominee or beneficiary of a deceased member, Form 10D for pension, Form 10C in relevant pension-withdrawal or certificate contexts, and Form 5IF for EDLI insurance benefit. But the form names are only the surface layer.
The real question is whether the deceased member had a valid nomination, whether family details were updated, whether the establishment is active or closed, whether Aadhaar and identity details match, and whether the claimant is the nominee, widow, child, other family member, or legal heir stepping in without a clean nomination trail. Post-death PF cases often become document-heavy faster than families expect. That is why a widow or family member with partial records should not assume that a death certificate alone will unlock the entire PF file.
How do I find old mutual fund investments in my name?
Old mutual fund tracing usually starts with PAN, folio clues, bank statements, email records, and any old account statement or distributor trail. AMFI and MF Central surface tracing routes including MITRA for inactive or unclaimed mutual fund folios, and there are separate checks for unclaimed dividend and unclaimed redemption amounts. RTAs such as CAMS or KFintech may also matter depending on which AMC held the folio.
A major reason people fail at this search is that they assume every old investment will appear neatly in one place. Some investments were made in physical or statement-of-account mode, some pre-date cleaner KYC discipline, some have address or bank changes, and some sit not as active units but as unclaimed payouts. “Find my mutual funds” therefore often means tracing both the folio and the state of the money linked to that folio.
How do I claim mutual funds after the death of a parent or spouse?
This is generally a transmission exercise first and a payout or redemption question second. The claimant has to establish the holder’s death, the claimant’s identity, bank details, and the route by which the units or proceeds should move. The document burden can include death certificate, KYC set, bank proof, transmission request, and in some cases indemnity, NOC, or succession papers depending on nomination status, holding mode, and value thresholds followed by the AMC or RTA.
One important misconception needs to be killed early: a nominee in mutual funds is not automatically the unquestionable final owner in every legal sense. AMFI’s investor material expressly notes that nomination does not by itself create beneficial title and that the nominee may receive as trustee for legal heirs or legatees. So a family should not confuse operational transmission with final succession certainty.
What is the difference between an inactive folio, an unclaimed redemption amount, and an unclaimed dividend in mutual funds?
They sound similar, but they are not the same problem. An inactive folio suggests an investment record that still exists but has become dormant, hard to trace, or operationally stale. An unclaimed redemption amount usually means units were already redeemed but the payout did not reach the investor correctly. An unclaimed dividend means the dividend was declared but remained unpaid or uncashed.
This distinction matters because the solution changes. One route may be folio tracing and KYC correction. Another may be proving entitlement to an already-generated payout. Another may require transmission after death. Treating all mutual fund problems as “missing money” is too broad. The real fix begins once you identify whether the issue is still units, already-triggered proceeds, or a stale record that has not been regularised.
How do I reactivate a dormant or inoperative bank account?
In ordinary cases, reactivation means approaching the bank, completing the required KYC update, and making the account operational again. RBI guidance treats savings and current accounts as inoperative when there are no customer-induced transactions for over two years. The same regulatory material also states that there are no charges for activation of an inoperative account.
If the account has remained unclaimed long enough, the deposit may have moved into the DEA framework and become searchable through RBI’s UDGAM system, but even then the claim or reactivation does not happen through RBI itself. It happens through the relevant bank. That distinction matters. Search and claim are different steps. The portal may help you find the trail, but the bank still has to regularise the customer and settle or reactivate the account.
How do I claim money from a deceased person’s bank account or fixed deposit?
The first question is whether there is a valid nominee or survivorship instruction. RBI’s customer-service guidance says that where there is a nomination or survivor clause, banks should not insist on succession certificate, probate, or similar legal representation merely to discharge the bank’s liability, provided identity and death are properly established and there is no court restraint. But the same guidance also clarifies that payment to the nominee is as trustee for the legal heirs. That means settlement convenience and final family entitlement are not identical questions.
Where there is no nomination, unclear records, or a dispute among family members, the documentary burden grows. Then the bank may need a stronger claimant trail, and the family may need to solve the succession problem before the money can move safely. In fixed-deposit cases, people also forget to check whether the money is still inside the bank as a live liability, already matured and left unclaimed, or already transferred into the unclaimed-deposit framework.
How do I check unclaimed deposits in old bank accounts?
Banks are required to display the list of unclaimed deposits or inoperative accounts that have remained inactive for ten years or more on their websites, and RBI’s UDGAM portal provides a central search facility across participating banks. If the search is successful, the result provides a UDRN and points the customer toward the relevant bank’s claim or reactivation route.
What UDGAM does not do is settle the claim. That part still happens through the bank. So if someone asks, “Can I recover the money directly from RBI?”, the answer is no. RBI helps with traceability and the framework; the bank remains the settlement point. This is a good example of why so many users feel stuck: the internet gives them a portal name, but not the difference between traceability, KYC, legal entitlement, and actual settlement.
Can an NRI claim old Indian shares, insurance, PF, mutual funds, or bank deposits from abroad?
Yes, but NRI cases are usually heavier than resident cases. The issue is not only whether the asset can be claimed. The issue is whether the execution chain can be completed remotely in a form the institution will accept. That often means one or more of the following: PAN and KYC alignment, overseas identity proof, notarisation or apostille, passport and visa or OCI evidence where relevant, NRE or NRO banking considerations, couriering signed originals, remote co-ordination with branches or registrars, and dealing with different formats across institutions.
NRIs also run into an extra pattern: the family believes the case is blocked because they are abroad, when the deeper reason is actually old-record mismatch, incomplete nomination, or missing entitlement proof. Distance worsens friction, but distance is often not the only problem. The right route starts with diagnosing whether the real obstacle is geographic, documentary, legal, or all three together.
What if my name, signature, address, PAN, bank, or KYC details do not match old records?
This is one of the most common reasons genuine cases stall. Old financial records often preserve an older spelling, pre-marriage surname, old signature style, outdated address, obsolete bank account, or incomplete KYC. When money is due years later, institutions do not see “same person, obvious case”; they see “record mismatch, payout risk”. That is why people with genuine money due still get stuck in repeated document requests.
The fix is rarely just one affidavit. Different asset classes want different correction sequences. Sometimes identity correction has to happen first. Sometimes claimant status has to be established first because the original holder has died. Sometimes bank proof becomes the choke point. A good file therefore solves mismatch as a sequence problem, not as a pile-of-documents problem.
What if I only have partial papers and not the full account number, folio number, policy number, or UAN?
Partial papers are common, especially in family-held old money cases. A single old share certificate, a premium entry in a bank statement, a passbook copy, a policy bond cover, a PF number on a salary slip, a dividend warrant, or an email from an AMC may be enough to begin reconstruction. Do not make the mistake of waiting for a perfect file before taking the first classification step. Perfect files are rare in old cases.
That said, partial papers do not guarantee a successful claim. They may make a case traceable without yet making it claim-ready. That distinction matters. Traceability answers “can we identify the asset trail?” Claimability answers “can the right claimant prove entitlement and complete the institution’s requirements?” The first can often begin with fragments. The second usually needs a stronger file.
How long do these claims usually take?
The honest answer is that old financial recovery work does not move on one universal timeline. A clean bank reactivation with intact KYC is not the same as a death-related PF claim with missing nomination. A straightforward insurer payout is not the same as a survival-benefit case tied up in old address and bank mismatch. A company-side share correction is not the same as post-death IEPF recovery.
Time is usually driven by five things: how clear the asset trail is, how clean the claimant position is, whether the records match, whether more than one institution is involved, and whether the case is being handled from India or from abroad. Anyone offering one neat universal number for all such matters is either overselling or has not understood the work.
Why do cases get stuck even when the money is genuine?
Because “money exists” is not the same as “institution can safely release it”. Most delays are not moral disputes. They are classification and documentation problems. The institution may not know whether the claimant is the right person, whether the identity matches the old record, whether the payout route is current, whether nomination is valid, whether family consent is needed, or whether the asset is still with the institution or already in a regulator-managed transfer system.
That is why repeated complaints often fail. A complaint is useful when the route is correct and the institution is simply delaying. It is much less useful when the route itself is still wrong, incomplete, or badly documented. In those cases, the case does not need louder words. It needs cleaner structure.

A person can and should handle simple matters directly where the route is clear. But many cases stop being simple long before the family admits it. By the time someone is searching three different portals, arguing with a bank branch, and trying to understand whether a nominee can sign for IEPF or PF or an insurance payout, the problem is usually no longer “where do I complain?” It is “what is this case, who can claim it, and what sequence will actually move it?”
